# Open-Ended Theme Coding Guide

Use this guide to summarize `biggest_weakness` and `improvement_suggestion` responses after the survey closes. The goal is not to overcomplicate the qualitative work; it is to make the theme summary transparent and consistent.

## Coding Steps

1. Read all open-ended responses once without coding.
2. Create a copy of the exported responses for coding.
3. Add two columns: `weakness_theme` and `improvement_theme`.
4. Assign one main theme to each response. If a response mentions several issues, choose the issue that seems most central.
5. Count the number of responses in each theme.
6. Use one or two short anonymized examples only if they add meaning and do not identify a student.

## Suggested Themes

| Theme | Use When Responses Mention |
|---|---|
| Low awareness | Students do not know services exist or hear about them too late |
| Scattered information | Internship information is split across too many channels |
| Late communication | Deadlines or opportunities are shared too close to due dates |
| Limited employer connections | Students want more companies, alumni, clinics, startups, or industry partners |
| Limited school-specific guidance | Students want advice tailored to their school or field |
| Limited interview preparation | Students want mock interviews or interview practice |
| Limited CV/application support | Students want faster or more practical CV, resume, or application help |
| Limited personalization | Students want one-to-one advising or follow-up |
| Tracking/support after applying | Students want application tracking, feedback, or follow-up after submitting applications |
| Other | The response does not fit the themes above |

## Reporting Format

Use a table like this in the results section:

| Theme | Count | Percentage | Example Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Low awareness | n | % | Students may need earlier and clearer communication |
| Scattered information | n | % | A central platform may reduce search effort |

## Caution

Do not report open-ended responses as if they represent all KIU students. They show common themes among the surveyed respondents and should be used to support, not replace, the quantitative findings.
